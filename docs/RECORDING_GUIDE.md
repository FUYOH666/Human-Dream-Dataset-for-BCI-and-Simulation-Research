# Dream Recording Guide

Practical recommendations for high-quality dream capture, based on 9 months of daily recall training.

---

## When You Wake Up (Night or Morning)

Dream content fades rapidly — a significant portion can be lost within minutes. Record **immediately** upon waking, before getting out of bed or switching context.

**Tools:** Notebook and pen by the bed, or phone with voice recorder / notes app.

---

## Quick Capture: Three Core Tags

If you wake at night and are too drowsy for a full account, capture at minimum:

| Tag | What to note |
|-----|--------------|
| **Place** | Where did it happen? (building, landscape, room, undefined space) |
| **Events** | What happened? (1–3 key actions or plot points) |
| **Participants** | Who was there? (people, creatures, yourself, no one) |

These three anchors often suffice to reconstruct the full dream later. Even a few words per tag can prevent total loss.

---

## Full Recording: As Detailed as Possible

When you have time (morning, or after a night wake-up):

- Record **everything** you remember — sensations, dialogue, objects, emotions, transitions between scenes.
- Don't filter or edit. Raw detail matters for pattern analysis.
- If a fragment feels meaningless, note it anyway. Connections may appear later.

---

## Voice vs. Writing

**Voice recording** is recommended when possible:

- **Speed:** Speaking is faster than writing. You capture more content before memory decays.
- **Encoding:** Verbalizing engages additional processing — you "replay" the dream in language, which reinforces retention.
- **Flow:** Hesitations, repetitions, and emotional tone are preserved; these often signal important material.

**Writing** works well too, especially if you prefer it or wake in a context where voice is impractical. The critical factor is **immediacy** — record as soon as you wake.

**Workflow used in this dataset:** Voice notes upon waking → later transcribed to text.

---

## Summary

1. Record immediately (notebook or phone).
2. Minimum: Place, Events, Participants.
3. Full account: as detailed as possible.
4. Preferred: voice recording for speed and encoding benefits.
